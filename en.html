Ocean Guardian - Namibia Marine Conservation Team


Hi everyone! We are Ocean Conservancy Namibia (OCN), an NGO established in Walvis Bay, Namibia. About 1.5 million rare sea lions live along the coast of Namibia. They are threatened by illegally discarded fishing gear and other marine debris entangled and tortured to death. Our main goal is to end the problem of entanglement of marine wildlife on our coasts. Article: World Environment Day - Meet the Namibian Sea Guardians who help sea lions out of trouble

World Environment Day——Meet the Namibian Ocean Guardian who helped sea lions out of trouble
Author: Ma Peimin Source: International Online

Today (June 5) is World Environment Day, and this year World Environment Day focuses on solutions to plastic pollution. In the coastal areas of Namibia, there are about 1.5 million sea lions living. The marine garbage generated by human economic and social activities seriously threatens the living environment of these marine animals. Discarded plastic, once wrapped around a sea lion's neck, is hard to come off. How can local environmental groups help these sea lions get out of trouble?

There are about 60,000 sea lions living in the Daigo Cape area of Walvis Bay, Namibia. What many people don't know is that sea lions are very playful and like to play with anything they can find like a child. For example, fishermen often use lines made of strong plastic in order to catch more fish. Once these discarded fishing lines are wrapped around the sea lion's neck, it is difficult to get off. As the sea lion grows up, the fishing line will become tighter and tighter around its neck, threatening its life.

Nord Dreyer, the founder of the Namibia Marine Conservation Team, and other members of the team, come to Daigo Cape and other areas at least 3 times a week. With excellent observation skills, teamwork and rich experience, they will lock the sea lions bound by ropes very accurately every time. Nord Dreyer said: "We have a lot of experience. We stay on the beach for hours at a time. We use binoculars to watch the sea lion colony, look for the entangled sea lion, and once we lock the target, we will run or Crawling over, we will also use kayaks and other technical means to make our work better."

Speaking of the origin of the establishment of the Marine Conservation Team, Nord Dreyer told reporters that he used to be a local tourist guide and often encountered sea lions when he went out to sea And so on when marine animals get trapped. "When I see sea animals struggling, it makes me sad as a human being. I jump out of the kayak, cut the rope, and set the sea animals free. Later, we found that this happened more and more, so in In 2020, we established the Namibia Marine Conservation Team."

Helping sea lions and other marine animals is now an important part of Nord Dreyer's life. Completing this task requires multiple devices and teamwork. So far, they have rescued more than 3,200 sea lions. Dreyer said: "Some of the small sea lions we can assist with bare hands. If they are about a year old, we use this small mesh bag, run over and hold them in the back, and this big mesh bag can hold them. Sea lions weighing up to 200 kg have two zippers, which require two people to work together. The other person opens the zipper, finds the entangled object, and solves it. This is a means of protecting ourselves, because these animals are very strong, and once bitten Serious."

Despite taking many measures, Dreyer admitted frankly that due to unpredictable situations, his own injuries are inevitable. But with his love for the ocean, he remains passionate about the cause. He and his team posted the video of helping animals out of trouble on social media around the world, which aroused huge attention. In a few days, he and his team will launch a documentary on social media to record the work of the past two years and arouse more people's awareness of protecting the earth and the environment. "The whole point of the documentary is to educate the public about the impact of plastic on the environment, we have to change our behavior because there is no magic button to take back plastic. We can start with small things around us, we must protect the ocean, and it is for ourselves The fate of the future."

Article: Protecting Marine Animals - Namibia Marine Conservation Team

Protecting Marine Animals—Namibia Marine Conservation Team
Authors: Chen Cheng, Deng Min Source: Xinhua News Agency

More than 200,000 Cape fur seals live in the Namibian coastal cities of Walvis Bay and Swakopmund. There is a group of volunteers who are affectionately called "Seal Team" by Chinese netizens. They have been active in different locations along the coastline for a long time, using homemade tools to rescue Cape fur seals that are troubled by floating marine garbage such as fishing nets, fishhooks, and plastic bags. .

The Namibia Marine Conservation Team was established in 2020. It is a non-governmental organization. It has expanded from the initial small team of three to the current seven-member team, all of whom live nearby volunteer.
